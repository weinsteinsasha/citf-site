Who I am

I'm from St. Petersburg, based in Cyprus. For twenty years I've run businesses in the creative world — and the whole time I've been building living things out of nothing that go on working without me.

In St. Petersburg I founded Part Academy — the country's largest performing-arts academy — and the independent theatre venue Skorohod. After moving, I built the Cyprus International Theatre Festival from scratch in four months; in three seasons it became the island's main theatre event. I've brought Malkovich, Serebrennikov, Chulpan Khamatova and Petrushevskaya to my stages. This October — Fanny Ardant with Cassandre.

Honestly, I just love talented people — probably why art always pulled me in: the concentration of bright minds there can be staggering. And I love interviewing them, taking apart how they're wired. One of those conversations was my open talk with John Malkovich.

I'm an engineer-economist by training — which explains why a spreadsheet and a piece of dramaturgy excite me equally. I'm a Fulbright Fellow: I taught in the US at the University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee and gave guest lectures at Berkeley, the University of Hawaiʻi at Mānoa and the University of Central Florida. I speak three languages fluently and have lived between countries my whole life — which, I think, makes me decent at translating people to one another.

How I can help

I'm always building my own things — I'm a practitioner, not a theorist at a podium. And what I enjoy most is helping others out of a crisis: it's what I'm good at and what I love.

Right now I advise several organizations — very different fields, teams of ten to a hundred people. They have one thing in common: a crisis always looks the same. Panic, fog, and everyone putting out fires in the wrong place.

In that fog I see the root — not a symptom like "our ads aren't working," but what's underneath it. Most often, someone in a crisis first needs to sort out their goals: where they're actually headed and what the business really stands on. Then comes focus (what to stop doing) and automation where it's genuinely needed. The industry doesn't matter — a crisis is built the same way everywhere.

I'm not a fan of the word "consultant" — I'd rather do it together than advise from the sidelines (Jobs seemed to feel the same). I'm mentored by Alexander Laryanovsky on business and Andrey Ozhigin on AI, and I mentor other founders on crisis management — it runs both ways.

FAQ

What does "crisis manager" mean?
I come in when everything's on fire or stuck. I quickly separate symptom from root, find the one point the way out hinges on, and help build a plan. Tools range from plain focus to AI.
Does the industry matter?
No. Art, education, IT, retail — a crisis looks the same everywhere. I look not at the sector but at what's actually broken.
A consultant who's never done anything himself?
Quite the opposite. I built the academy, the venue and the festival with my own hands, from scratch. I'm a practitioner — and I see consulting as an exchange, not a lecture.
How much, and how do we try it?
It starts with a free 30-minute chat — Calendly above. First we see whether there's anything for me to fix at all. If there is — we discuss the format.
